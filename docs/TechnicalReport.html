Technical Report

Abstract

A prominent topic over the past decade has been the continued rise in prices of single-family homes in America. Prices of housing contribute to wealth inequality and perpetuate barriers to family formation and upward mobility, which negatively affect many crises that the United States of America currently faces. Our hope with this project was to gain some empirical insight into why housing has become less accessible for young Americans in recent years. Using a fixed-panel regression model, we find that within-tract increases in median household income and the owner–renter income gap are the predictor variables most strongly associated with rising ownership costs.

Introduction

Over the past decade, a prominent social and economic issue has been the lack of accessibility to housing. The notion of the American Dream suggests that all citizens should be afforded the privilege of owning a house provided they work hard and manage money accordingly, but in recent years, this dream has become less realistic for many Americans.

While it would have been ideal to look into single-family home purchase accessibility for the entirety of the United States of America, given the constraints we were working with, we decided to focus on data from Utah, found through the US Census.

The primary goal of this analysis is to identify which tract-level factors are most strongly associated with changes in monthly owner costs over time in Utah.

Data Acquisition and Wrangling

Data was obtained through the US Census Bureau’s American Community Survey (2009-2023). We constructed a dataset spanning 772 census tracts across Utah, which includes 393 ZIP codes and 29 counties.

When considering an outcome variable for this analysis, we picked monthly owner costs for households with a mortgage as an indicator of how much it costs a Utahan to purchase and live in a home. Predictor variables were chosen based on easily quantifiable measures, like numbers of units and average income, and measures available through the US Census American Community Survey.

We decided to pull these eight tables, at a granularity of each census tract, each year, for years 2009-2023.

Table Details

B25001 Housing Units
B25003 Tenure (Owner Occupied vs Renter Occupied)
B25008 Total Population in Occupied Housing Units by Tenure
B25119 Median Household Income in the Past 12 Months (in 2024 Inflation-Adjusted Dollars) by Tenure
B25032 Tenure by Units in Structure
B25088 Median Selected Monthly Owner Costs (Dollars) by Mortgage Status
B19013 Median Household Income in the Past 12 Months (in 2024 Inflation-Adjusted Dollars)

Model Selection and Validation

We built a two-way fixed effects (or fixed panel) regression model to identify within-tract drivers of ownership costs.

We initially wanted to use a simple multiple linear regression model, but found that we were explaining almost none of the variance in our data. The poor performance of the simple model is likely due to omitted variable bias from unobserved, time-invariant tract characteristics like general desirability and proximity to jobs. A fixed effects model helps to remedy the inevitable unknown elements of variance in such an economic model, as it holds certain elements constant to account for inherent variance.

Our model controls for tract-level fixed effects, meaning time-invariant characteristics of a neighborhood like location and county \(\times\) year fixed effects, which account for unknown variables like employer expansion and COVID-era impacts. Our model also excludes the years 2009-2013 to account for the economic recovery following the 2008 recession. The model assumes that housing costs within a tract would follow the same trend as other tracts in the same county and year.

The model is as follows:

\[ y_{it} = \beta X_{it} + \alpha_i + \lambda_{c(i), t} + u_{i,t} \]

where \(i =\) tract (Census GEOID), \(t =\) year, and \(c(i) =\) county of tract \(i\).

Because tract fixed effects remove all time-invariant differences across tracts and county \(\times\) year effects remove shocks common to a county in a given year, the coefficients \(\beta\) are identified from within-tract deviations over time relative to other tracts in the same county and the same year. For example, the coefficient on median household income reflects how changes in income within a tract over time, compared to other tracts in the same county and year, are associated with changes in owner costs.

\(\alpha_i\) stands for tract fixed effect, which controls for anything constant within a tract over time, like location desirability, zoning baseline, and neighborhood quality. In other words, \(\alpha\) lets us compare a tract to itself over time. This means that all estimates are identified using within-tract changes over time and not differences between tracts.

\(\lambda_{c(i), t}\) stands for the county \(\times\) year fixed effect, controlling for anything affecting a county in a specific year; things like local economic shocks (ex: big employer entering or leaving), policy changes, or impacts from COVID. In other words, \(\lambda_{c(i), t}\) allows us to remove shared shocks across nearby areas at the same time.

Model Building

Our model is implemented in Python and uses features of the statsmodels package as well as the linearmodels package.

We first filter the dataset to relevant variables, these being tract, county, year, monthly owner costs for households with a mortgage, percent of single-family homes that are renter-occupied, median household income, owner-renter income gap, percent of vacant single-family homes, and total population in tract.

The owner–renter income gap is defined as the difference between median owner household income and median renter household income within a tract-year, and percentage variables are computed as proportions of relevant housing unit counts within each tract. Median household income captures general purchasing power across tracts, while population and vacancy rates capture demand.

Note that we need each census tract to appear in at least two time periods to ensure we can identify the within-tract variation.

We next construct the regression design matrix. Fixed effects are incorporated using an absorbing approach rather than explicit dummy variables, which allows us to easily scale to thousands of tracts and county-year combinations. Two sets of fixed effects are included: tract fixed effects and county \(\times\) year fixed effects, which are constructed by interacting county and year into a single categorical variable. These absorbing effects are passed to the AbsorbingLS model as categorical variables, allowing the estimator to partial them out efficiently during estimation. The model is fit using clustered standard errors at the tract level.

In short, the estimation procedure follows these steps:

  1. Prepare and clean the dataset
clean = _prepare(df, PREDICTORS)
clean["county_year"] = clean["county"].astype(str) + "_" + clean["year"].astype(str)
  1. Construct predictor matrix \(X\) and outcome \(y\)
X = sm.add_constant(clean[PREDICTORS])
  1. Define absorbed fixed effects (tract and county-year)
absorb = pd.DataFrame({
    "tract_fe":       pd.Categorical(clean["GEOID"]),
    "county_year_fe": pd.Categorical(clean["county_year"]),
})
  1. Estimate the model via AbsorbingLS and compute cluster-robust standard errors at the tract level
model = AbsorbingLS(clean[OUTCOME], X, absorb=absorb)
result = model.fit(cov_type="clustered", clusters=pd.Categorical(clean["GEOID"]),)

Analyses, Results, and Interpretation

Key findings

  • Median household income and the owner-renter income gap are the strongest predictors of increases in owner costs.

  • Other variables like vacancy and the percentage of single-family homes that are renter-occupied are not statistically significant after the including of fixed effects.

A table depicting the results of the fixed panel model is below.

Fixed Effects Model (Tract FE + County×Year FE)

Variable Coef Std. Err. t-stat p-value CI Low CI High
pct_sf_renter_occupied -103.558 64.051 -1.617 0.106 -229.097 21.981
median_household_income 0.0018 0.0004 4.851 0.000 0.0011 0.0026
owner_renter_income_gap 0.0006 0.0002 3.925 0.000 0.0003 0.0009
pct_vacant 31.207 72.176 0.432 0.666 -110.258 172.673
pop_in_occupied_total 0.0088 0.0046 1.934 0.053 -0.0001 0.0178

Observations: 8,284
Absorbed R²: 0.9455

We see that a $10,000 increase in median household income within a tract is associated with an approximately $18 increase in monthly owner costs when holding tract, and county \(\times\) year effects constant. Similarly, a $10,000 increase in the owner-renter income gap is associated with an approximately $6 increase in monthly owner costs.

The complexity of this model leads to a lot of things to consider. For example, while “Absorbed \(R^2\)” is impressively high, the county \(\times\) year fixed effect will by nature increase the \(R^2\) metric. So, we need to rely on coefficient stability and statistical significance rather than the \(R^2\). We found after fitting two different models (the first being a one-way fixed effects model) that main variables were more stable in our current model. Additionally, although not individually statistically significant, the other variables remain part of the joint explanatory structure of the model and contribute to overall fit.

In particular, the coefficients on median household income and the owner-renter income gap remained consistent in both magnitude and statistical significance, suggesting that these relationships are not driven by broader county-level shocks. Overall, this stability across model specifications provides evidence that our results are not sensitive to unobserved, time-varying county-level factors, and supports the use of the more complex model as a framework for identifying within-tract drivers of housing costs.

These findings directly address our research goal by identifying which tract-level changes are most strongly associated with rising ownership costs over time.

Conclusions

We were successfully able to establish that variables strongly associated with the rise in house prices in Utah within tracts over time are median household income, the owner-renter income gap, and population growth, answering our original question of what factors are leading to a difficulty in purchasing homes in recent years. It should be noted that these estimates are really of association and not causation.

While there are still many questions we would like to explore, like “how do zoning laws affect house prices?” and “is it really less profitable to build less expensive homes?”, we consider this analysis to provide useful insight into the drivers of housing costs.
